# Strategy Skill

Your job is to produce a genuinely useful strategy — not a list of goals,
not a vision statement, not a motivational plan. A real strategy diagnoses
a specific challenge and identifies a coherent set of actions that, taken
together, have a realistic chance of overcoming it.

The single most important thing to understand: **most "strategies" are
actually bad strategies in disguise**. Before building anything new,
you need to know whether that's what you're dealing with.

---

## Step 1 — Understand the situation

Before any strategic thinking, make sure you understand the problem space:

- **What is the entity trying to achieve?** (company, team, person, product)
- **What challenge or obstacle stands in the way?** Not goals — the actual
  friction, competition, or constraint that makes this hard.
- **What resources, capabilities, or asymmetries exist?** What does this
  entity have that rivals or the situation do not?
- **What has been tried before, and why didn't it work?**

If the user is vague, ask one focused clarifying question. The diagnosis
cannot be done without real facts about the situation.

---

## Step 2 — Diagnose the challenge

This is the hardest and most important step. A good diagnosis:

- **Names the specific nature of the challenge**, rather than restating the
  goal. "We want to grow revenue" is not a diagnosis. "Our customer
  acquisition cost has tripled because our primary channel is saturated
  and we have no secondary channel" is a diagnosis.
- **Identifies the critical constraint or pivot point** — the one or two
  factors that, if addressed, would unlock the most progress. Not every
  problem needs solving; only the ones that are both critical and
  addressable deserve focus.
- **Simplifies complexity** without falsifying it. A diagnosis is a
  judgment call — it says "this aspect of the situation is the key one."
  It will be wrong sometimes, but a crisp diagnosis that turns out to be
  slightly wrong beats paralysis or vague platitudes.

Ask yourself: if this diagnosis is correct, what would solving *only* this
problem unlock? If the answer is "not much," the diagnosis is too narrow.
If solving it would unblock everything else, you've likely found the
right lever.

---

## Step 3 — Check for bad strategy first

Before building a strategy, explicitly check whether you're looking at bad
strategy. Bad strategy has four hallmarks — if any of these are present in
the user's existing plan, name them directly:

**1. Fluff** — Language that sounds strategic but says nothing. Phrases like
"world-class innovation," "leverage our synergies," "customer-centric
excellence," or "transformational growth" with no specific meaning. If you
can't say what the words rule *out*, they're fluff.

**2. Failure to face the challenge** — The strategy avoids stating what the
actual problem is. This usually happens because naming the real challenge
forces uncomfortable choices. A strategy that doesn't name a specific
challenge cannot be evaluated, improved, or acted upon.

**3. Goals mistaken for strategy** — "We will grow 40% this year" is not a
strategy. "We will become the market leader" is not a strategy. These are
desired outcomes. Strategy is the *approach* to achieving them — the logic
of how you intend to get there, given your specific situation and constraints.

**4. Bad strategic objectives** — Objectives that either don't address the
diagnosed challenge, or that are so vague or grandiose they can't guide
action. "Delight our customers" is not an objective. "Reduce checkout
abandonment from 68% to under 40% by Q3" is one.

If you find bad strategy, say so clearly and explain which hallmarks are
present. Then proceed to build a good one.

---

## Step 4 — Build the kernel

Every good strategy has a three-part kernel. Work through each element:

### A. Diagnosis
A crisp statement of the critical challenge. One or two paragraphs at most.
It should name:
- The key obstacle or constraint
- Why it exists (root cause, not symptom)
- What makes this the right place to focus rather than others

### B. Guiding Policy
The overall approach for dealing with the diagnosed challenge. This is not
a to-do list — it's an orientation, a set of principles that rule *in* some
actions and *out* others. A guiding policy explains *how* the organization
intends to position itself or move relative to the obstacle.

A good guiding policy creates *coherence* — it makes the actions below feel
like they belong together rather than being a random list of initiatives.
It often exploits an asymmetry: something the entity has or can do that
rivals cannot easily replicate.

Ask: "Does this policy focus effort, or does it try to do everything at
once?" If it's trying to please everyone, it's not a guiding policy — it's
a compromise.

### C. Coherent Actions
The specific steps that carry out the guiding policy. These should:
- Work together and reinforce each other (not be independent parallel tracks)
- Follow logically from the guiding policy
- Be realistic given actual resources
- Be sequenced if order matters
- Be concrete enough that someone could execute them

A useful test: if you removed the guiding policy, would these actions still
make sense together? If yes, they're probably not coherent — they're just
a task list.

---

## Step 5 — Check for sources of power

Good strategies draw on at least one real source of leverage. Review which
of these are in play and make sure the strategy exploits them:

**Leverage** — Is there a pivot point where concentrated effort produces
outsized results? The best strategies apply force to a single critical
constraint, not everywhere at once.

**Proximate objectives** — Is the nearest target close enough to actually
hit? Bold distant goals are inspiring but not actionable. The strategy
should specify a reachable next milestone that the organization can
"overwhelm."

**Asymmetry** — What does this entity have that rivals lack? Advantage is
rooted in differences. If the strategy ignores the entity's specific
strengths and weaknesses relative to others, it is generic and therefore
weak.

**Rivals' inertia** — Are competitors slow or constrained in ways that
create an opening? Large organizations are often unable to respond quickly
due to routines, culture, or prior commitments. A good strategy exploits
this window before it closes.

**Dynamics** — Is there a wave of change underway (technology shift,
regulatory change, buyer behavior shift) that this strategy can ride? The
best timing for a strategy is when the landscape is in flux and old
positions of strength are up for grabs.

**Chain-link weakness** — Is there a single limiting factor whose removal
would unlock everything else? If so, the strategy should fix that link
rather than strengthening others that don't matter yet.

---

## Output format

Present the strategy as a clear, structured document:

```
SITUATION SUMMARY
─────────────────
[2–3 sentences on the entity, its goal, and its context]

DIAGNOSIS
─────────
[The critical challenge: what it is, why it exists, why it's the right
lever to pull. 1–3 paragraphs.]

GUIDING POLICY
──────────────
[The overall approach. What it focuses on, what it rules out, what
asymmetry it exploits. 1–2 paragraphs.]

COHERENT ACTIONS
────────────────
1. [Specific action — brief explanation of why it follows from the policy]
2. [Specific action — brief explanation]
3. [Specific action — brief explanation]
...

SOURCES OF POWER IN PLAY
────────────────────────
[Which leverage points this strategy uses and why]

WHAT THIS STRATEGY DOES NOT DO
────────────────────────────────
[The 2–3 things explicitly deprioritized, and why. This is important:
a strategy that doesn't exclude anything isn't a strategy.]
```

The final section — what the strategy does *not* do — is as important as
what it does. Strategy is fundamentally about choice. If nothing is being
sacrificed, no real strategic thinking has occurred.

---

## After presenting the strategy

Invite one round of critical feedback: "Does the diagnosis match what you
know about the situation? Are there constraints or resources I've missed?"

The diagnosis is always the most likely place to be wrong — the person
closest to the situation often has information that changes everything.
A revised diagnosis usually leads to a different guiding policy, which
changes the actions. Take this seriously rather than just adjusting
wording on the surface.

---

## What to avoid

- **Producing a vision/mission/values document.** That's not strategy.
- **Listing every possible initiative.** Comprehensiveness is the enemy
  of strategy. Force prioritization.
- **Using language that sounds strategic but commits to nothing.** If a
  phrase would apply equally well to any organization in any industry,
  cut it.
- **Treating goals as if they were a plan.** Always ask: "How, specifically,
  given this situation?"
- **Building a strategy around what the entity *wants* to be true** rather
  than what *is* true. The diagnosis must face reality honestly, including
  uncomfortable facts about weaknesses, market position, and past failures.
